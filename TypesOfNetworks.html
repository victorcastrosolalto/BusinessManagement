Types of Computing Networks

Understanding Network Classification by Size, Connectivity, and Data Sharing

Networks can be categorized based on their physical size, type of connectivity, and how data is shared. Understanding these classifications helps us design, implement, and manage effective network systems.

Networks by Size

Personal Area Network (PAN)

The smallest network type, connecting devices in close proximity to an individual.

Example: A phone wirelessly syncing data to a health watch or wireless headphones connecting to a smartphone.

Local Area Network (LAN)

Connects computers or devices on a single site, from a home network to large offices or schools.

Facilitates sharing resources like printers, centralized data stores, and internet access.

Example: A school network connecting classrooms, offices, and computer labs.

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

Covers a larger geographical area, typically a city (5-50 km).

Example: New York City provides "free Wi-Fi to all residents as well as wirelessly connecting traffic lights and parking meters."

Wide Area Network (WAN)

The largest networks spanning wide geographical areas, connecting multiple LANs.

The internet is the "largest WAN accessible to the public."

Example: A multinational corporation connecting offices worldwide using VPNs or leased lines.

Types of Connectivity

Wired Networks

Traditionally use copper Ethernet cables, now increasingly fiber-optic cables for better speed and distance.

Valued for their "reliability, faster speeds and ability to transfer data more securely."

Wireless Networks

Utilize Wi-Fi technology and radio signals, offering ease of setup and flexibility.

Devices connect via wireless access points (WAPs) or wireless routers, which are still connected to a wired backbone.

Cloud Networks

Integrate network capabilities on public or private cloud platforms.

Allows for off-site management of the network or data, simplifying deployment, saving costs, and enabling scalability.
