7. Internet Dilemmas: Risks and Consequences

The widespread use of networks and the internet introduces various risks and ethical dilemmas, requiring both individual and professional security measures.

7.1 Understanding the Risks to Data

Spamming

Sending unsolicited emails, primarily for advertising.

In March 2021, "45% of email traffic was spam."

Hacking

"Unauthorized access to a computer or network."

Social Engineering: Tricking users into revealing credentials. Example: Lithuanian national defrauded Google and Facebook of US$100 million.

Website/Network Penetration: Gaining access to backend databases.

Exploiting Security Flaws: Taking advantage of inadequate protection. Example: "Log4J attack" in 2021.

Viruses

Malicious software attached to programs or emails, activated upon execution.

2016 Bangladesh Bank Hack: North Korean Lazarus group utilized virus-laden CV attachment.

Ransomware

Malware that locks users out and demands payment.

WannaCry (May 2017): Affected 230,000 computers globally, causing US$4B in losses.

DDoS Attacks

Overwhelming a server with connection requests from bots.

Amazon Web Services experienced a major DDoS attack in 2020.

7.2 Consequences of the Risks to Data

Identity Theft

"When someone steals your personal information with the intention of committing fraud."

In 2020, identity theft cost Americans US$56 billion.

Anonymity

While offering protection, it also empowers those with malicious intent.

Internet Trolls: "People who leave intentionally provocative or offensive messages online." Studies show online harassment costs governments billions in health costs and lost income.

Data Retention and the Right to be Forgotten

Questions arise about data security after services are no longer used.

GDPR in Europe grants citizens the "right to request to have their data erased" under specific circumstances.

Surveillance

The close monitoring of individuals or groups using technologies like CCTV, facial recognition, drones, and smart devices.

A 2020 study found "16 out of the top 20 most surveilled cities are in China."

Government monitoring (e.g., COVID-19 trace apps) raises significant privacy concerns.
